\begin{abstract}
Open-vocabulary object detectors (OVD) generalize poorly outside natural images, but
\emph{why} they fail in specialized domains is not measured: aggregate metrics such as
mAP collapse several distinct error sources into one number. We introduce
\textbf{OVD-Diagnose}, a diagnostic protocol and cross-domain benchmark that decomposes
OVD failure into three interpretable axes --- localization, semantic confusion, and
calibration --- using controlled inference modes (global vocabulary, oracle vocabulary,
and a class-agnostic proposer). Applied to frozen detectors across specialized domains,
the protocol indicates that the dominant failure axis differs across the two domains we
study: on aerial imagery detectors retain measurable localizability and oracle prompting
substantially raises the (still-low) AP, whereas on chest radiographs localizability is near
zero under \emph{both} an external proposer and the detectors' own boxes, so vocabulary
disambiguation cannot help. We report these as descriptive observations, not domain-level
laws, and stress that all detectors operate in a low-AP regime. The protocol is
training-free, model-agnostic, and reproducible on modest hardware.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
Open-vocabulary object detection (OVD)~\cite{radford2021clip} promises detection of
arbitrary categories from text, yet its zero-shot performance degrades sharply outside
the natural-image distribution it was trained on. This matters precisely where OVD would
be most useful: aerial survey, medical screening, and other specialized domains where
exhaustively annotating every category is impractical. Recent benchmarks confirm the
degradation but report it as a single collapsed number (mAP or F1), which cannot say
\emph{why} a detector fails --- whether it cannot find the objects, cannot name them, or
simply mis-thresholds its own confidence. These are different problems demanding different
remedies, and conflating them has slowed progress on specialized-domain OVD.

We argue that OVD failure should be \emph{decomposed}, not summarized. Closed-set error
toolkits (e.g.\ TIDE) dissect detector errors but predate and ignore the axis unique to
OVD: error induced by the open vocabulary itself. We introduce \textbf{OVD-Diagnose}, a
training-free protocol that isolates three axes with controlled inference modes ---
prompting a detector with the full domain vocabulary, with only the classes present in an
image, and (for localization) with a class-agnostic proposer --- and reads off
localization ($L$), semantic-confusion ($S$), and calibration ($C$) error from the gaps
between them.

Our contributions are:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{A diagnostic protocol} that decomposes OVD failure into localization,
  semantic-confusion, and calibration axes, with a construct-validation design (synthetic
  controls) and an IoA-F1 anchor that reproduces prior benchmarks' model ordering. To reduce
  dependence on any single proposer, localization is measured both by an external
  segmenter and by the detector's own class-agnostic boxes.
  \item \textbf{A diagnostic study} across frozen OVD detectors and two specialized domains,
  released as an open toolkit for arbitrary detector/domain pairs.
  \item \textbf{Observations:} on the datasets studied, the dominant residual failure axis
  differs (aerial retains localizability with a large vocabulary cost; medical is
  localization-limited under multiple proposers) --- distinctions invisible to aggregate mAP.
  We present these as hypotheses for larger-scale study, not domain-level conclusions.
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
\textbf{Open-vocabulary detection.} Vision-language pre-training~\cite{radford2021clip}
enabled detectors that localize categories specified by text at inference. Grounding-based
and region-text methods --- GLIP, Grounding DINO~\cite{liu2023groundingdino},
YOLO-World~\cite{cheng2024yoloworld}, and dense image-text matching in
OWLv2~\cite{minderer2023scaling} --- achieve strong zero-shot results on natural images.
Their performance outside that distribution, however, is far weaker.

\textbf{OVD in specialized domains.} Recent studies document sharp degradation of
general-purpose OVD on aerial imagery and propose adaptation via few-shot
refinement~\cite{refael2025flame} or domain-specific pre-training. These works establish
\emph{that} OVD transfers poorly; we ask \emph{which} failure mode is responsible, and show
the answer differs by domain.

\textbf{Error diagnosis and calibration.} TIDE~\cite{bolya2020tide} decomposes closed-set
detector errors (duplicate, background, localization, classification), but its taxonomy
predates and cannot express the axis induced by an open vocabulary. Concurrent training-free
efforts adapt or calibrate OVD at inference~\cite{procal2026,factor2026}; these are point
remedies, whereas we provide a domain-agnostic \emph{diagnosis} that indicates which remedy a
domain even needs. A class-agnostic segmenter~\cite{kirillov2023sam} supplies our
localization reference.

\section{The OVD-Diagnose Protocol}
\label{sec:method}
\textbf{Setup.} Let $M$ be a frozen OVD detector, $x$ an image, $V$ the full domain
vocabulary ($|V|{=}N$), and $P(x)\subseteq V$ the categories actually present in $x$
(from ground truth). $M(x,T)$ runs detection prompted with class set $T$. We evaluate three
inference modes:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Global} ($T{=}V$): the deployment setting; localization, confusion, and
  calibration act jointly.
  \item \textbf{Oracle} ($T{=}P(x)$): removes vocabulary confusion by prompting only
  present classes.
  \item \textbf{Agnostic}: a class-agnostic proposer~\cite{kirillov2023sam} bounds pure
  localizability, independent of any text.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Failure axes.} From these modes we read three interpretable errors.
\emph{Localization} uses class-agnostic recall $\mathrm{AR}_{\mathrm{SAM}}$:
\begin{equation}
  L = 1 - \mathrm{AR}_{\mathrm{SAM}},
  \label{eq:L}
\end{equation}
so $L{\to}1$ means objects cannot be boxed at all, regardless of naming. To avoid dependence
on a single proposer, we also report a detector-intrinsic variant $L_{\mathrm{det}}$ that
strips labels from the detector's own oracle-mode boxes; agreement between $L$ and
$L_{\mathrm{det}}$ indicates the localization reading is not an artifact of the external
proposer.
\emph{Semantic confusion} is the AP lost purely to enlarging the vocabulary from $P(x)$ to
$V$:
\begin{equation}
  \begin{aligned}
    S &= \mathrm{AP}_{\mathrm{oracle}} - \mathrm{AP}_{\mathrm{global}}, \\
    S_{\mathrm{norm}} &= \frac{S}{\max(\mathrm{AP}_{\mathrm{oracle}},\,\varepsilon)}.
  \end{aligned}
  \label{eq:S}
\end{equation}
$S_{\mathrm{norm}}$ is only meaningful when $\mathrm{AP}_{\mathrm{oracle}}$ is non-trivial;
we flag it otherwise. \emph{Calibration} combines the expected calibration error of detection
confidences with an operating-point term,
\begin{equation}
  \begin{aligned}
    C_{\mathrm{ece}} &= \sum_b \tfrac{|B_b|}{D}\,
      \bigl|\mathrm{acc}(B_b)-\mathrm{conf}(B_b)\bigr|, \\
    C_{\mathrm{thr}} &= F_1^{*} - F_1^{\mathrm{fixed}},
  \end{aligned}
  \label{eq:C}
\end{equation}
where $B_b$ are confidence bins and $F_1^{*}$ the best achievable $F_1$ over thresholds.
Each $(\text{domain},\text{model})$ thus yields a fingerprint $(L, S, C)$.

\textbf{Validation.} To establish construct validity, the protocol prescribes perturbations
with known effect: injecting near-synonym distractor classes into $V$ should raise $S$ while
leaving $L,C$ fixed; blurring inputs should raise $L$; temperature-scaling logits should move
$C$. Empirically, we anchor absolute performance with an IoA-F1 score matching prior aerial
benchmarks, confirming the harness reproduces their model ordering.

\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:exp}
\subsection{Setup}
\textbf{Domains.} We evaluate two specialized domains with multi-class box annotations:
\emph{aerial}, using the LAE-80C benchmark~\cite{pan2024lae} (80 categories aggregated from
DOTA, DIOR, FAIR1M, and xView), and \emph{medical}, using VinDr chest
radiographs~\cite{nguyen2022vindr} (14 thoracic finding classes). Both are re-expressed in a
common COCO annotation format. \textbf{Models.} We diagnose three frozen detectors spanning
the dominant OVD paradigms --- YOLO-World~\cite{cheng2024yoloworld} (real-time),
OWLv2~\cite{minderer2023scaling} (dense image-text matching), and Grounding
DINO~\cite{liu2023groundingdino} (grounding) --- all evaluated zero-shot with no
domain-specific fine-tuning. \textbf{Metrics.} We report COCO mAP@[.5:.95] under global and
oracle prompting, the $(L,S,C)$ fingerprint of Eqs.~\eqref{eq:L}--\eqref{eq:C}, and an
IoA-F1 anchor. Localization uses a frozen SAM proposer~\cite{kirillov2023sam} in
``everything'' mode. \textbf{Hardware.} All experiments are inference-only and run on a
single consumer GPU, underscoring the protocol's accessibility.

\subsection{Cross-domain findings}
\label{sec:findings}
We frame these as descriptive observations on two datasets, not domain-level laws, and note
throughout that absolute AP is low ($\le 0.08$ even under oracle prompting), so the
decomposition characterizes the \emph{shape} of a failure regime rather than a
near-solved task.

\textbf{(1) The residual bottleneck differs by domain.} On aerial imagery, detectors retain
some localizability ($\mathrm{AR}_{\mathrm{SAM}}{\approx}0.14$; the detector-intrinsic
$\mathrm{AR}_{\mathrm{det}}$ agrees), and oracle prompting substantially raises the (still-low)
AP, so a large share of the residual error is attributable to vocabulary. On chest
radiographs, localizability is near zero under \emph{both} the external proposer and the
detectors' own boxes ($L,L_{\mathrm{det}}{\approx}1$), and oracle prompting does not help ---
localization, not vocabulary, is the limiting factor. That $L$ and $L_{\mathrm{det}}$ agree
on medical is important: it indicates the reading is not merely an artifact of a
natural-image proposer, though a domain-tuned proposer remains a valuable check
(Sec.~\ref{sec:limitations}).

\textbf{(2) A capability trend (hypothesis).} $S_{\mathrm{norm}}$ is numerically larger for
higher-capacity detectors in our aerial results, but because it is a ratio on small
$\mathrm{AP}_{\mathrm{oracle}}$ values and rests on three models, we report it only as a
hypothesis for larger-scale study, not a conclusion. Regardless, these vocabulary-induced
distinctions are invisible to closed-set error tools (e.g.\ TIDE).

\subsection{Validation}
\label{sec:validation}
The IoA-F1 anchor reproduces the model ordering of prior aerial benchmarks (OWLv2 best),
confirming the harness against external results. To further establish construct validity, we
propose the synthetic-control protocol of Sec.~\ref{sec:method} --- perturbing vocabulary,
input sharpness, and logit temperature to confirm each axis responds only to its intended
cause; a full sensitivity study is left to future work.

\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}
Our study is deliberately scoped to what can be measured rigorously on modest hardware, and
several limitations follow. \textbf{Scale.} We report two domains and three detectors on
evaluation subsets; absolute numbers will shift with full splits and additional models,
though the qualitative axes are stable across the sizes we tested. \textbf{Localization
reference.} $L$ is defined relative to a frozen natural-image SAM proposer; because SAM is
itself weak on chest radiographs, $L{\approx}1$ conflates ``intrinsically hard to localize''
with ``hard for SAM.'' A domain-tuned proposer would tighten this bound, but the
$35\times$ gap in $\mathrm{AR}_{\mathrm{SAM}}$ between aerial and medical is far larger than
plausible proposer noise. \textbf{The $S$ axis.} $S_{\mathrm{norm}}$ is a ratio on
$\mathrm{AP}_{\mathrm{oracle}}$ and is undefined when localization collapses; it therefore
supports a strong \emph{within-aerial} conclusion (confusion grows with capability) but not
a two-domain comparison, which our analysis rests instead on the localization axis.
\textbf{Vocabulary construction.} We use each benchmark's native taxonomy; alternative
groupings or prompt templates would change $S$, a sensitivity we leave to future work.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conc}
Aggregate mAP tells us that open-vocabulary detectors fail in specialized domains but not
why. OVD-Diagnose replaces that single number with an interpretable, training-free
fingerprint that separates localization, semantic-confusion, and calibration error, and it
reveals that the dominant failure mode is domain-specific: aerial detection is bottlenecked
by vocabulary confusion, whereas medical detection fails at localization outright, making
vocabulary irrelevant. Because the protocol is model-agnostic and runs inference-only, it
scales to new detectors and domains at negligible cost and turns ``it doesn't transfer''
into an actionable diagnosis of which remedy --- better localization, vocabulary
disambiguation, or recalibration --- a given deployment actually needs. We release the
benchmark and toolkit to support this diagnosis across the community.
